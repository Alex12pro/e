Overview

A focused reference on how tornadoes develop, how they are rated, how risk is communicated, and which events shaped tornado history.

What a tornado is

A tornado is a violently rotating column of air in contact with the ground and attached to a thunderstorm. Many of the strongest tornadoes come from supercells, which are long-lived rotating thunderstorms with a mesocyclone detected on radar.

Rotating thunderstormGround contactExtreme local winds
Supercell linkMost large and violent tornadoes are associated with supercells.
Not fully solvedNOAA notes that tornado formation is still not completely understood in detail.

Tornado season at a glance

In U.S. climatology, May usually has the highest count of tornado occurrences, June is second, and April is third. Tornadoes can still happen during any month if the atmosphere lines up the right way.

2024 and 2025 activity snapshot

NOAA reported a record-setting May in 2024, and the annual 2025 preliminary total reached 1,559 tornado reports, well above the 1991–2020 annual average baseline.

How tornadoes form

Simple science first, then the storm-scale details behind supercell tornadoes.

Step-by-step formation

1

Warm, moist air rises

Thunderstorms feed on buoyant air near the ground. Moist unstable air helps power strong updrafts.

2

Wind shear creates spin

Changing wind speed and direction with height can create horizontal tubes of rotating air.

3

The updraft tilts that spin upright

Inside a supercell, the storm's updraft tilts the rotation into the vertical, helping build a mesocyclone.

4

Low-level inflow tightens the rotation

Warm, moist air feeding the storm near the ground can intensify the lower-level circulation.

5

A tornado forms at the surface

If the near-ground rotation strengthens enough and connects with the cloud-base circulation, a tornado can develop.

Why supercells matter

NOAA explains that the most destructive tornadoes occur from supercells. A supercell has a rotating updraft called a mesocyclone, and that larger storm-scale rotation can exist well before a tornado forms.

MesocycloneA broad rotating updraft that can be miles wide and can show up on Doppler radar before a tornado forms.
Storm-relative inflowAir streaming into the storm near the surface helps supply moisture and momentum to the circulation.
DowndraftsRear-flank and forward-flank downdrafts can help reshape the near-ground environment and influence whether tornadogenesis happens.
Still an active science problemResearchers understand the broad setup, but NOAA still states the exact details of tornadogenesis are not fully understood.

Forecasting vs detection

Tornado forecasting starts with identifying environments favorable for severe thunderstorms. If the setup is right, the Storm Prediction Center can issue a watch. Detection happens closer to event time using radar, spotters, environmental clues, and warning decisions by local National Weather Service offices.

What makes tornadoes dangerous

Tornadoes account for only a tiny fraction of a thunderstorm's total energy, but that energy is focused into a very small area. The result is concentrated destruction from wind, flying debris, and rapid changes in direction and intensity.

Enhanced Fujita Scale

The EF Scale has been operational since February 1, 2007 and rates tornadoes using estimated wind speeds inferred from damage.

How the scale works

The Enhanced Fujita Scale compares observed damage against standard damage indicators and degrees of damage. The result is an estimated wind-speed range and a rating from EF0 to EF5. It is based on damage, not direct wind measurement.

EF0

65–85 mph · Light

Branches break, signs are damaged, and shallow-rooted trees may be pushed over.

EF1

86–110 mph · Moderate

Roofs are stripped, mobile homes can overturn, and vehicles may be pushed off roads.

EF2

111–135 mph · Considerable

Roofs are torn from homes, large trees are snapped, and mobile homes are demolished.

EF3

136–165 mph · Severe

Stories of well-built homes are heavily damaged, trains may overturn, and forests are shredded.

EF4

166–200 mph · Devastating

Well-constructed houses can be leveled and vehicles become dangerous missiles.

EF5

201+ mph · Incredible

Strong-frame homes can be swept away and heavy structures suffer extreme destruction.

Important limits

Not the same as measured windEF ratings are inferred from damage and engineering judgment, not usually from instruments placed in the tornado.
Construction quality mattersHow a structure is built can strongly affect damage, which is why the EF scale uses multiple indicators and damage levels.
Old F-scale vs EF-scaleThe original Fujita scale was replaced operationally in the U.S. on February 1, 2007.
Rare top-end tornadoesEF5 tornadoes are the most violent category and are extremely rare relative to weak tornadoes.

Record book

A curated set of major official U.S. tornado record categories and standout reference points.

Major U.S. records

Deadliest tornado on record

695 deaths

The March 18, 1925 Tri-State Tornado across Missouri, Illinois, and Indiana is widely cited as the deadliest tornado in U.S. history.

Longest path length on record

219 miles

The same 1925 Tri-State Tornado is also cited in official references for the longest tornado path length on record.

Highest recorded tornado wind speed

318 mph

Mobile Doppler radar measured 318 mph winds aloft in the May 3, 1999 Bridge Creek/Moore, Oklahoma tornado. NOAA notes this is the highest recorded tornado wind speed.

Widest tornado on record

2.6 miles

The May 31, 2013 El Reno, Oklahoma tornado exceeded the previous Hallam, Nebraska width mark and is cited in official NOAA references as 2.6 miles wide.

Deadliest single U.S. tornado since 1950

158 deaths

The May 22, 2011 Joplin, Missouri EF5 tornado is cited by NOAA as the deadliest single tornado since 1950.

Largest Oklahoma outbreak on record

1999 outbreak

NOAA describes the May 3, 1999 event as the largest tornado outbreak ever recorded in Oklahoma.

Record-setting May tornado count

May 2024

NOAA reported that May 2024 set a new record for the highest May tornado count, surpassing the previous May record.

Recent annual total well above average

1,559 in 2025

NOAA's 2025 annual tornado report said preliminary tornado reports reached 1,559, or 127% of the 1991–2020 annual average. NOAA's 2024 climate summary separately noted at least 1,735 confirmed tornadoes in 2024.

Historic events

Several outbreaks changed warning practices, building awareness, and the public memory of tornado risk.

Timeline

March 18, 1925 · Tri-State Tornado

Historic F5 assessment, 219-mile path, and roughly 695 fatalities. It remains the deadliest tornado on U.S. record lists.

April 3–4, 1974 · Super Outbreak

One of the most famous outbreak sequences in U.S. history, with dozens of violent tornadoes across multiple states.

May 3, 1999 · Oklahoma/Kansas outbreak

Produced the Bridge Creek/Moore F5 tornado where the highest recorded tornado wind speed was measured by mobile radar.

April 25–28, 2011 · Super Outbreak

A devastating multi-day event that included numerous violent tornadoes across the Southeast and Midwest.

May 22, 2011 · Joplin, Missouri

An EF5 tornado killed 158 people, making it the deadliest single U.S. tornado since 1950 in NOAA references.

May 31, 2013 · El Reno, Oklahoma

A massive, complex tornado reached a record width of 2.6 miles in official NOAA references.

Why these matter

Historic tornadoes are not just records. They are case studies used in meteorology, engineering, emergency management, radar research, and public safety training.

Radar researchBridge Creek/Moore 1999 reshaped how the public understands extreme wind measurements.
Warning policyMajor outbreaks drove investment in watches, warnings, spotter networks, and communication systems.
Damage engineeringPost-storm surveys help improve how damage indicators are used in EF ratings.
Preparedness cultureEvents like Joplin made sheltering and interior-room messaging much more visible.

Safety and alerts

Knowing the language matters. Watches and warnings are not the same thing.

Alert terms

Tornado WatchConditions are favorable for tornadoes and severe thunderstorms in and near the watch area. Use the time to review your plan.
Tornado WarningA tornado has been indicated by radar or reported by trained observers. You need to take shelter immediately.
Tornado EmergencyA rare, enhanced wording used when a confirmed, life-threatening tornado is moving into a populated area and catastrophic damage is expected.

Best shelter basics

Go lowGet to a basement if possible, or the lowest floor of a sturdy building.
Go interiorChoose a small interior room, hallway, or closet away from windows.
Protect your headUse your arms, a helmet, mattress, or thick blankets to reduce injury from debris.
Vehicles are unsafeCars and mobile homes are especially dangerous in tornadoes. Leave for stronger shelter well before the storm arrives.

Night tornado problem

Tornadoes at night are especially dangerous because they are harder to see and can catch sleeping people off guard. Redundant alerts matter: weather radio, phone warnings, local alerts, and a shelter plan.

Misconceptions to avoid

"Open the windows" is a mythDo not waste time opening windows. Get to shelter immediately.
Overpasses are dangerousWind can accelerate through them and debris can become deadly.
Tornadoes can occur outside classic Tornado AlleyThey happen in many regions, including the Southeast, Midwest, Plains, and beyond.
